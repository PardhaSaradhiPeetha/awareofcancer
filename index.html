Understanding Cancer

Cancer is a complex group of diseases characterized by the uncontrolled growth and spread of abnormal cells. These cells can form tumors, invade nearby tissues, and spread to other parts of the body through the blood and lymph systems (a process called metastasis).

Cancer can start in almost any tissue or organ of the body. While not all tumors are cancerous (malignant), malignant tumors can invade and destroy healthy tissues. Early detection through screening and adopting healthy lifestyle habits significantly improve survival rates and quality of life.

Knowledge, awareness, and regular screening save lives.

Types of Cancer

Cancers are typically named for the organs or tissues where they form. Here are some of the most common types:

Important Note

This list provides general information about cancer types. If you have concerns about any symptoms or risk factors, please consult with a healthcare professional for personalized medical advice.

Breast Cancer

Develops in breast tissue, most commonly in the ducts that carry milk to the nipple or the glands that produce milk. Includes invasive ductal carcinoma, invasive lobular carcinoma, and inflammatory breast cancer.

Lung Cancer

Begins in the lungs and is strongly linked to smoking. The two main types are non-small cell lung cancer (more common) and small cell lung cancer (more aggressive).

Prostate Cancer

Develops in the prostate gland in men. It's often slow-growing but can be aggressive in some cases. Regular screening is important for early detection.

Colorectal Cancer

Starts in the colon or rectum, often developing from precancerous polyps. Screening tests can find and remove polyps before they become cancerous.

Skin Cancer

The most common type of cancer. Includes basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma (the most dangerous form).

Blood Cancers

Includes leukemia (cancer of blood-forming tissues), lymphoma (lymph system cancer), and multiple myeloma (plasma cell cancer).

Common Cancer Symptoms

While these symptoms can be caused by conditions other than cancer, it's important to consult a healthcare professional if you experience any persistent or unusual changes in your body.

Unexplained Weight Loss

Losing 10 pounds or more without trying could be a sign of various cancers, especially those of the pancreas, stomach, esophagus, or lung.

Fatigue

Extreme tiredness that doesn't improve with rest can be an early sign of some cancers, like leukemia or cancers that cause blood loss.

Persistent Pain

Ongoing pain without a clear cause that doesn't go away may signal bone, brain, or other cancers.

Skin Changes

Any changes in size, shape, or color of moles or other skin spots, or new skin growths should be examined.

Causes and Risk Factors

Cancer develops due to changes (mutations) in DNA that control cell function. These changes can be inherited or caused by exposure to carcinogens. Search below to filter causes or keywords like "tobacco", "alcohol", "radiation", etc.

Tobacco Use

The leading preventable cause of cancer worldwide. Smoking causes lung, throat, mouth, pancreatic, bladder, and many other cancers. Avoid all forms of tobacco and secondhand smoke.

Alcohol Consumption

Linked to breast, liver, colorectal, esophageal, and head and neck cancers. The risk increases with the amount consumed. Limit alcohol intake or avoid it completely.

UV Radiation

Excessive exposure to ultraviolet radiation from the sun or tanning beds significantly increases skin cancer risk. Use SPF 30+ sunscreen, wear protective clothing, and avoid peak sun hours.

Unhealthy Diet

Diets high in processed meats, red meat, and low in fruits and vegetables may increase cancer risk. Focus on a balanced diet rich in plant-based foods.

Carcinogenic Materials & Safety Levels

Understanding the carcinogenic potential of common materials can help you make safer choices. The bars below represent relative risk levels based on scientific evidence.

Understanding Risk Levels

Risk levels are based on the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classifications and represent the strength of evidence linking a substance to cancer, not necessarily the level of risk in everyday exposure. Always follow safety guidelines for any potentially hazardous materials.

Tobacco Smoke

High Risk

Contains at least 70 known carcinogens. Directly linked to lung, throat, mouth, and many other cancers.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Avoid all tobacco products
  • Stay away from secondhand smoke
  • Seek help to quit if you currently smoke

Asbestos

High Risk

Once widely used in construction. Inhalation of fibers can cause mesothelioma and lung cancer.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Leave asbestos removal to professionals
  • Use proper respiratory protection if exposure is possible
  • Be aware of asbestos in older buildings

Processed Meats

Medium Risk

Classified as Group 1 carcinogen by IARC. Regular consumption increases colorectal cancer risk.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Limit consumption of processed meats
  • Choose fresh alternatives when possible
  • Balance diet with plenty of fruits and vegetables

Alcohol

Medium Risk

Classified as Group 1 carcinogen. Risk increases with amount consumed.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Follow recommended limits (1 drink/day for women, 2 for men)
  • Consider alcohol-free days each week
  • Be aware of increased breast cancer risk with alcohol consumption

BPA (Bisphenol A)

Low Risk

Found in some plastics and food can linings. Potential endocrine disruptor with limited evidence of carcinogenicity.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Choose BPA-free products when possible
  • Avoid heating plastic containers
  • Use glass or stainless steel for food storage

Charred Meat

Low Risk

Cooking meat at high temperatures can create heterocyclic amines and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons.

Safety Recommendations:

  • Avoid charring or burning meat
  • Use lower temperature cooking methods
  • Marinate meat before grilling

Prevention & Healthy Lifestyle

While not all cancers can be prevented, research suggests that up to 40% of cancers could be prevented through healthy lifestyle choices and environmental changes.

Healthy Eating Habits

  • Eat plenty of fruits and vegetables (aim for 5+ servings daily)
  • Choose whole grains over refined grains
  • Limit processed and red meats
  • Reduce sugar and salt intake
  • Maintain a healthy weight

Physical Activity

  • Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate exercise weekly
  • Include strength training twice weekly
  • Reduce sedentary time - stand up and move regularly
  • Find activities you enjoy to maintain consistency

Support for Those Affected by Cancer

A cancer diagnosis affects not just the patient but also their family and friends. Support systems are crucial for emotional well-being and treatment success.

Emotional Support

  • Join support groups (in-person or online)
  • Consider counseling or therapy
  • Practice stress-reduction techniques
  • Communicate openly with loved ones

Practical Support

  • Help with daily tasks and appointments
  • Assist with medical information organization
  • Provide transportation to treatments
  • Help with meal preparation
